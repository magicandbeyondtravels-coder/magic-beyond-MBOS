# Brand Voice

The canonical definition of how Magic & Beyond Travels communicates across every customer touchpoint.

This object governs expression, not strategy.

Business strategy is defined in [mission.md](mission.md), [positioning.md](positioning.md), [brand-promise.md](brand-promise.md) and [differentiators.md](differentiators.md).

## Voice principles

1. **Organize before you inform.** The mission is to move families from overwhelm to confidence through education and planning systems ([mission.md](mission.md)). Every piece of writing should reduce the number of things a reader has to hold in their head, not add another option to weigh.
2. **Confidence over hype.** The brand earns trust by being specific and calm, not by overselling destinations — see "what we are not" in [differentiators.md](differentiators.md).
3. **Simplify the decision, not the trip.** Per [brand-promise.md](brand-promise.md), copy should make a choice easier, not make the destination sound bigger.
4. **Speak to one family, not an audience.** Voice should read like it's addressing the specific overwhelmed family in front of it, never a broadcast crowd.
5. **Write toward the transformation.** Every message should nudge the reader from the "before" state toward the "after" state defined in [customer-transformation.md](customer-transformation.md) — overwhelmed and unsure, toward confident and clear.
6. **Teach before you sell.** Every interaction should leave the customer understanding something they did not understand before.

## Tone

Tone sits at a consistent point on each of these spectrums:

- **Formal ↔ Casual:** warm and plainspoken. Not stiff and corporate; not slangy or gimmicky.
- **Serious ↔ Playful:** calm and reassuring. Not somber; not jokey when a family is trying to make a real decision.
- **Distant expert ↔ Peer:** an informed guide walking alongside the family. Not a detached authority; not a fellow tourist with no more expertise than the reader.

## What the brand should sound like

- A knowledgeable friend who already did the research.
- Organized: clear options, clear next step, never an open-ended pile of information.
- Specific and concrete, not vague travel-blog inspiration.
- Warm but efficient — respectful of a family's time and mental load.
- An experienced educator explaining a complex subject in simple language.

## What the brand should not sound like

- A pushy travel agent closing a sale.
- A generic travel blog chasing superlatives.
- A corporate call-center script.
- An influencer selling a destination rather than a decision.
- Anything that adds to the overwhelm this business exists to remove (see [positioning.md](positioning.md), "core problem").

## Preferred language

Decide, confidence, clarity, plan, narrow down, together, family, straightforward, ready, "here's what I'd suggest."

## Avoid

Bucket list, epic, dream vacation, once-in-a-lifetime used as filler, stacked superlatives ("the most amazing, unforgettable..."), manufactured urgency ("book now before it's too late"), and unexplained industry jargon (fare classes, rate codes).

## Writing rules

Whenever possible:

- Use plain language.
- Prefer short paragraphs.
- Explain complex ideas with examples.
- Avoid unnecessary adjectives.
- Give a clear recommendation.
- Reduce cognitive load.
- End with a clear next step.

## Before and after phrasing

| Before | After |
| --- | --- |
| "Check out these AMAZING once-in-a-lifetime Disney deals!!!" | "Comparing Disney options for a family of six? Here's how I'd narrow it down." |
| "So many incredible cruise options, the choices are endless!" | "Cruises get overwhelming fast. Let's cut the options down to three that actually fit your family." |
| "Book now before prices go up!" | "Here's what I'd lock in now, and what's safe to decide later." |
| "We can't wait to help make your dream vacation a reality!" | "We'll help you make the call with confidence, not just help you book a trip." |

## Channel guidance

Voice adapts by channel, but the throughline stays fixed to the stage of the [customer journey](../customers/customer-journey.md) each channel typically serves.

### Social media
Mostly Discover and Learn. Lead with the decision problem, not destination inspiration. Short and concrete; avoid the generic travel-influencer aesthetic entirely.

### Email
Spans Trust through Return. Warmer and more personal than social. Every email should move the reader one step closer to a decision, never sit as pure filler content.

### Website
Must work for a first-time visitor in Discover and a returning client in Return. Lead every page by helping the reader answer their next question.

### Consultations
The warmest, most conversational register, delivered live. Same principles apply, but this is where organizing the decision should feel most present — walking a family through a clear framework, not chatting about destinations.

## Never

Never:

- Shame a family for not knowing something.
- Pressure a family into booking.
- Use fear as a sales tactic.
- Assume Disney is the right answer.
- Speak as if every family wants the same vacation.
